Tutorial Contents

This interactive simulator lets you design, build, and test electric circuit—like structures from the popular video game Minecraft.

If you have not played Minecraft before, or are unfamiliar with how redstone works, I recommend you read the section at the bottom titled "What is Redstone?" first. Then, check the controls and examples sections.

The system of redstone can be somewhat confusing at times. While it is possible to learn it only by reading this tutorial, if you are interested in it, you might be better off watching a few YouTube videos first.

Controls

  • You can move around by using the WASD and QE keys, and rotate the camera with the arrow keys.

  • The location that you're currently looking at is highlighted in yellow. Pressing Space will either remove the block at that location, or place a block when the location is empty.

  • The yellow toolbar on the bottom shows which block you are going to place. You can change which one you have currently selected by pressing the 1–4 keys.

Blocks

This simulator implements a few of the blocks that can be used to build redstone circuits.

Please note that this section is intended as reference for people who already know the redstone system. As such, it is much longer and more detailed than the other sections.

  • Cube: The standard cube block; can be selected via 1 on the keyboard. Cubes can be used to build structures, or serve as scaffolding for other blocks.

  • Redstone wire: Used to transport signals from one place to another; can be selected via 2 on the keyboard.

    A block of wire connects to adjacent wires and can thus be used to transport a signal from one location to another. (Wire cannot travel diagonally.) By placing wire laterally adjacent but one block higher or lower, you can build "stairs" that let signals travel up or down.

    Redstone wire must be mounted on top of a solid surface to be placed — i.e. it must be on a cube or a slab.

    An individual block of wire is either unpowered and appears very dark red (its usual state), or is powered by an adjacent powered block and appears appear red.

    The power in a length of wire will fall off over distance, and weaker-powered wire will appear darker. After 15 blocks, the power will fade out completely.

  • Slab: a "half-cube"; can be selected via 3 on the keyboard.

    Slabs fulfill most of the same functions as cubes, but have some slight differences. Slabs cannot be powered (in the sense that a cube is). First, Redstone torches can only be mounted on the top side of a slab.

    Second, when redstone wire is placed on "stairs" made of slabs, the wire will not visually connect and power will only be able to travel in the "downwards" direction of the stairs.

    Lastly, slabs cannot be used to "pinch" stairs of redstone wire.

  • Redstone torch: a toggleable power source; can be selected via 4 on the keyboard.

    Redstone torches must be mounted on a solid surface; in practice this means that you can only place torches on the top or sides of a cube, or the top of a slab. (Torches can't be placed on a downwards-facing surface.)

    Redstone torches can be either lit ("on") or unlit ("off"). Lit torches will provide power to any adjacent blocks, such as wires.

    In addition, a lit torch will strongly power any cube block directly above it, meaning that wires placed on that cube will also be powered.

    Whether a torch is turned on or not depends on whether the block it is mounted on is weakly powered.

    A cube, the most common mounting block for torches, is weakly powered when either there is a lit torch directly underneath it, or there is a powered wire whose shape is pointing into the cube. (Slabs cannot be weakly powered, which effectively means that a torch mounted on a slab can never be turned off.)

What is Redstone?

Redstone is a group of fictional devices from the popular video game Minecraft. It serves as a loose analogue of electricity and can be used to create simple devices like light switches to more complicated things like combination locks or automated factories and machines.

Although each individual "redstone device" is simple, when combined they can become (to some extent) nearly as versatile as real-life electronic circuits. Players have even been able to create fully-functional — albeit simple — computers running inside of Minecraft!

This simulator lets you experiment with several redstone devices and create simple circuits in your web browser. It doesn't have every single tool and feature as the "real" Minecraft, but you can still use it to quickly try your ideas at any time.
